The Impact of Education on Employment and Income

Understanding how educational qualifications influence career outcomes, skill levels, and earnings in the Australian workforce

Education is widely recognized as a key driver of economic opportunity and social mobility. This visualization explores the relationship between educational qualifications and employment outcomes in Australia, examining how different levels of education correlate with job skill levels, occupational categories, and earning potential.

Using data from the Australian workforce, we analyze patterns across postgraduate degrees, bachelor's degrees, diplomas, certificates, and workers without formal qualifications to understand which educational pathways lead to higher-skilled positions and greater financial rewards.

Executive Summary

This analysis reveals how educational qualifications shape employment outcomes across Australia's workforce through four key dimensions: skill levels, industries, occupations, and demographics.


Higher qualifications enable access to higher-skilled jobs, with Skill Level 1 positions paying nearly triple Skill Level 5 roles. Bachelor and postgraduate degrees dominate the highest skill tiers, though skill level relevance varies significantly by industry—healthcare prioritises Skill Level 1 while retail emphasises Skill Level 5.


Educational returns depend heavily on industry context. Mining offers premium earnings across all qualifications, while service sectors show minimal educational premiums. Managers and Professionals—the highest-paid occupations—are dominated by degree holders, whereas vocational certificates support middle-income trades and technical roles.


Higher education correlates strongly with employment stability, with postgraduate degrees achieving 76% full-time employment rates. Gender patterns show females dominating higher education while males lead vocational training. Education follows predictable life patterns, with bachelor degrees peaking at ages 18-19 and postgraduate studies rising through the mid-20s.

The evidence demonstrates that educational investment pays dividends through access to higher-skilled, better-paid work, though returns vary substantially by industry choice and occupational pathway.

Job Skill Level and Job Industry

The relevance of the 5 different skill levels across 19 different industries

Skill level relevance across industries

This stacked barchart shows the amount of people possesing each of the different 5 skill levels across the 19 different industries. In essence it depicts which skill levels are most/least common for each industry, which acts as a proxy for the most/least relevant skill levels. For example as per the visualisation in the health care and social assistance industry, the most common skill level is 1 and the least common is 3, whereas in the retail trade industry the most common skill level is 5 and the least common is 3. This emphasizes that there isn't a universally common/important skill level, skill level 1 might be the most relevant in some industries whilst also being the least relevant in other industries, therefore it's important to recognise which skill level is most common in the industry relevant to you before determing the education qualification most relevant to that skill level.

Key Insights

  • Skill level importance varies by industry: Healthcare and social assistance shows Skill Level 1 as most common and Skill Level 3 as least common, while retail trade shows the opposite pattern—Skill Level 5 dominates and Skill Level 3 is least common. This demonstrates there is no universally "important" skill level across all sectors.
  • Industry-specific patterns matter for career planning: Before determining which educational qualification to pursue, identify which skill level is most prevalent in your target industry. A qualification relevant for high-skill positions in one industry may be less valuable in sectors dominated by different skill requirements.

Education and Job Skill Level

How educational qualifications relate to the skill requirements of jobs

Qualification Composition by Skill Level

This heatmap shows the percentage distribution of 7 different educational qualifications across 5 skill levels. Skill Level 1 represents the highest-skilled jobs whilst Skill Level 5 represents lower-skilled positions. The color intensity indicates what proportion of workers at each skill level hold each type of qualification.

Key Insights

  • Higher education leads to higher earnings through skill level: Bachelor and postgraduate degrees account for 73% of Skill Level 1 positions, which earn nearly 3x more than Skill Level 5 ($1,944 vs $700 weekly). This demonstrates the direct pathway from education to higher-skilled, better-paid work.
  • Skill level relevance is industry-specific: While Certificate III/IV qualifications dominate Skill Level 3 positions (63%), the importance of each skill level varies by industry. Healthcare emphasizes Skill Level 1, while retail prioritizes Skill Level 5—meaning career planning requires understanding both the qualification-to-skill relationship AND which skill levels your target industry values.
  • Educational pathways must align with industry needs: Before investing in a qualification, identify which skill levels dominate your target industry, then determine which education credentials lead to those skill levels. A postgraduate degree valuable for Skill Level 1 healthcare roles may be less relevant in retail sectors where Skill Level 5 (most commonly held by Certificate III/IV holders) is most prevalent.
  • Postgraduate credentials are highly specialized for top-tier roles: While postgraduate degrees show strong relevance at Skill Level 1 (25%), they decline sharply across other skill levels and are virtually irrelevant at Skill Levels 3-5. Similarly, Graduate Diploma/Graduate Certificate shows minimal relevance across all skill levels, indicating these are niche credentials for specific professional advancement rather than broad workforce entry.
  • Certificate III/IV is the versatile mid-level credential: This qualification shows significant representation across Skill Levels 3, 4, and 5, making it a flexible credential for various workforce entry points, while Certificate I/II and Certificate n.f.d. show minimal relevance across all skill levels.

Industry Earnings by Education

Average weekly earnings across industries for different qualification levels

Weekly Earnings by Industry and Qualification

Select an educational qualification level from the dropdown menu to see how average weekly earnings vary across different industries. This interactive chart reveals which industries offer the highest financial returns for each qualification type, and demonstrates how different education qualifications impact (increase or decrease) the weekly income someone can earn within each specific industry.

Key Insights

  • Mining leads in earnings: Consistently shows the highest average weekly earnings across most qualification levels ($3,400+ for postgraduates)
  • Industry variation matters: The same qualification can yield vastly different earnings depending on the industry sector
  • Lower returns in service sectors: Accommodation, food services, and retail consistently show lower earnings regardless of qualification
  • Higher qualifications don't always guarantee higher earnings: In mining, graduate diploma holders ($3,433) actually earn slightly more than postgraduate degree holders ($3,400), while bachelor's degree holders earn $2,742—demonstrating that educational progression doesn't always translate to proportional salary increases, particularly in industries where specialized diplomas may be more valued than research degrees.
  • Service sectors show minimal educational premiums: In accommodation and food services, postgraduate holders ($1,266) earn only $266 more weekly than Certificate III/IV holders ($1,000), and in retail the gap is just $174—indicating that pursuing higher education yields limited financial returns in these sectors compared to the $700+ premiums seen in mining and professional services.
